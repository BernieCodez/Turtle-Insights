Shells

The carapace and plastron both have two layers. One layer is flat bones that merge with the turtle's ribs as it grows. The other layer consists of scutes which protect the turtle. For most turtles, the scutes are made of keratin. But for Leatherbacks they have a rubbery material called caraplace with leather to protect them. A turtle shell is also the home to small organisms called epibionts. They are small creatures like algae, small crabs, barnacles, etc. There are over 100 epibionts discovered living on a sea turtle's shell. The weight of these creatures will weigh the turtle down but luckily certain fish enjoy eating the epibionts making it a win win for both sides. The turtle gets a clean shell and the fish gets a nice meal.

Senses

Sight

Turtles have vision that has adapted to both the land and the water although since a turtle spends most of its time in water, its vision underwater is better compared to land. They have perfect vision underwater and a slight nearvision on land. This is because their eyes have flat corneas and a nearly spherical lens. Interestingly, turtle eyes are more adapted to bright light instead of the dark. If they need to feed in the depths or in the dark they rely on seeing the bioluminesce of their prey to feed. A study in 2012 showed that green sea turtles and loggerhead turtles both have the cells required for seeing color but not many studies have been conducted to show whether they can distinguish the color they see.

Hearing

Turtle's ears are so small they can't be seen on the outside of its head. Their ear allows them to hear vibrations traveling through the water. Studies suggest turtles also have the ability to detect low frequency sounds because of a protective layer of skin over their ear called tympanum. Underneath the tympanum is a layer of fat only sea turtles have. Scientists believe the fat channels the low frequency sound waves into the ear and is what allows turtles to hear the loew frequency sounds.

Touch

An interesting fact about sea turtles is that they have sensory nerves inside of their shell that detect pressure. This means when their shells are touched they can detect it. Although scientists believe they can't detect pain. Turtles have sensory nerves in their head and flippers.

Smell

Turtles have an acute sense of smell. This combined with their sight allows them to easily track their prey. Scientists think that female turtles also use their sense of smell to navigate to a beach and lay eggs.

Magnetic sense

Turtles have a unique ability called magnetic sense. It allows them to detect magnetic fields that they use to navigate. Scientists aren't quite sure how it works. It is a turtle's equivalent to a compass.
